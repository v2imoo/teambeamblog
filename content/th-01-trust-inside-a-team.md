Of the eight dimensions of team health, trust is the one that gets talked about most and understood least. It sits underneath the other seven — communication, alignment, decision-making, all of them lean on it — and yet it is the hardest to point at, because trust is mostly visible in its absence.

It helps to be precise about what it actually is, because "we need more trust" is not an instruction anyone can act on.

## Two kinds of trust

There are two different things hiding inside the word. The first is competence trust: believing the people around you are good enough at their jobs that you can depend on their work without checking it. The second is vulnerability trust: feeling safe enough with your colleagues to be exposed in front of them — to say you were wrong, to admit you are lost, to disagree openly, to ask the question that reveals what you do not know.

Most teams have some of the first. Far fewer have the second, and the second is the one that decides how a team performs under pressure. A team can be full of capable people who all quietly protect themselves, and that team will be slow, cautious, and brittle exactly when it needs to be fast and honest.

## What low trust looks like

Low trust rarely announces itself. It shows up as friction that everyone blames on other things. People check each other's work instead of relying on it. Bad news travels late, because no one wants to be the one holding it. Disagreements happen in side conversations instead of in the room. Mistakes get hidden until they are too big to hide. Meetings are agreeable on the surface and unresolved underneath.

Each of these looks like a separate problem — a process issue, a communication issue, a personality issue. Underneath most of them is the same thing: people do not feel safe enough to be exposed, so they manage their exposure instead of doing the work.

## Why a speech does not fix it

The instinct is to address trust head-on — a session about vulnerability, a leader saying "this is a safe space." It does not work, because trust is not a belief you can install by talking about it. It is a conclusion people reach from evidence. You trust a team because you were wrong in front of them and it did not cost you. You asked for help and got it without a price. You disagreed and were still respected the next day.

That evidence accumulates in ordinary moments, and it accumulates fastest when a team goes through something together where being exposed is safe. This is why the right kind of shared experience builds trust that a workshop about trust cannot: it creates real instances of relying on each other and being vulnerable, and those instances are the evidence trust is built from.

## Building it, and knowing it took

Building trust deliberately means creating conditions where people depend on each other and are exposed in front of each other, in a setting where failure is survivable. Not manufactured risk, but genuine reliance, repeated, until the team has proof it can lean on itself. The leader's part is to go first — to be the one who admits the mistake, so everyone else learns it is allowed.

And trust can be read. You can see whether a team is safe to be honest in, and read it again later to see whether the safety held once the stakes rose. That reading matters more than most, because trust is the dimension that, when it moves, moves everything above it. Fix trust and communication improves on its own, decisions stick, and bad news arrives early enough to act on.

It is the base of the stack. Nothing built on top of low trust holds.
