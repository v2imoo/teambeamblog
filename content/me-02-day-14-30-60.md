The most common way to measure a team experience is a form at the end of the day. It asks whether people enjoyed it and whether they would recommend it. Those forms come back warm, because people who have just had a good afternoon feel good. Then the number gets filed as proof the thing worked.

It is not proof. It is a photograph of a mood, taken at the one moment mood is highest. The change you actually care about has not happened yet, because change is what is left after the mood is gone.

## Why one reading is not enough

A team experience creates a lift. The useful question is what the lift decays to. Some of it is pure glow and disappears within days. Some of it is a genuine shift in how people work together, and it holds. A single reading cannot tell these apart, because on the day they look identical. You need to read the team again, after the glow has had time to fade, and then again after normal work has had time to test whatever remains.

That is why we read at three points after an experience, not one.

## What each window is for

**Fourteen days.** This is the first honest reading. The afternoon is over, the group chat has gone quiet, and people are back in the run of work. Whatever shows up now survived the glow. If a shift in how openly a team talks, or how it makes a decision, is still visible at fourteen days, it is real rather than residual.

**Thirty days.** By now the team has hit normal pressure — a deadline, a disagreement, a bad week. Thirty days tells you whether the change holds when things get hard, which is the only condition under which it matters. Improvements that evaporate the first time work gets stressful were never improvements. Ones that survive a hard month are load-bearing.

**Sixty days.** This is the question of whether the change has become the new normal or is slipping back. At sixty days a genuine shift has either settled into how the team simply works now, or it has faded and the team has returned to its old baseline. Knowing which is what lets a leader decide whether to reinforce, repeat, or move on to a different gap.

We are not going to set out here what the reading consists of, because that belongs to the work and not to this page. The point is the cadence itself, and what the shape of the curve reveals.

## Reading the shape

Put three readings next to the starting point and a team's response takes on a shape. A sharp rise that falls away by fourteen days was a good day and nothing more. A smaller rise that holds through thirty and sixty days is a real change that will compound. A dimension that barely moves at first and then climbs is often a slow-burning shift in trust, which rarely announces itself immediately.

Those shapes are useful in a way a single number never is. They tell a leader not just whether something worked, but what kind of thing happened, and therefore what to do next.

## What a leader does with it

The cadence turns measurement from a verdict into a guide. It shows which changes are sticking and which are slipping, so reinforcement goes where it is needed rather than everywhere or nowhere. It gives the board a curve instead of a claim. And over several experiences it builds a picture of how a particular team responds, which makes every subsequent design sharper.

The day is the intervention. The fortnight, the month, and the two months after it are the proof. Skipping them is how a company spends on its teams for years and never learns whether any of it worked.
