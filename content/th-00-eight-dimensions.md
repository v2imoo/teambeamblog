Everyone wants a healthy team, and almost no one can say what one is. The phrase gets used the way "good culture" gets used — as a feeling everyone recognises and no one can pin down. The trouble with a feeling is that you cannot improve it. You can only improve something you can name, so we name it. We read a team across eight dimensions.

They are not a personality test and not a scorecard for individuals. They are the parts of how a group works together, each of which can be strong or weak on its own.

## The eight

**Trust.** Whether people believe each other's competence and intent enough to depend on one another without checking. It is the quietest dimension and the one everything else rests on.

**Communication.** Not how much a team talks, but how well the important things travel — whether the hard message gets said, and whether it lands.

**Alignment.** Whether people are actually pointed at the same thing. Teams are often busy and unaligned at once, which feels like progress and is not.

**Collaboration.** Whether people build on each other's work or run in parallel lanes that happen to share a building. Two functions can be cordial and still not collaborate.

**Decision-making.** Whether the team can decide and stay decided. A team that reopens every decision a week later is not slow, it is stuck.

**Energy.** Whether the team has the capacity to engage, or is running on empty. Energy is not enthusiasm; it is the fuel the other dimensions burn.

**Belonging.** Whether people feel part of the team or adjacent to it. It is the difference between someone who offers a half-formed idea and someone who stays silent to stay safe.

**Leadership.** Whether the people leading create the conditions the other seven need — safety to speak, clarity of direction, and the example that sets the team's weather.

## Why eight, and not one number

The temptation is always to collapse these into a single score, because a single number is easy to report and easy to compare. We resist it, for a reason that matters. A team is almost never uniformly healthy or unhealthy. It is strong on some of these and quietly hollow on one or two, and the hollow ones are doing most of the damage while the strong ones hide it.

A team can communicate constantly and not trust each other. It can be full of energy and completely unaligned. It can be close and warm and unable to make a decision. Average those into one number and the specific failure — the thing actually costing the team — vanishes into a middling score that tells you nothing about what to do. The point of the dimensions is not to grade the team. It is to find the one that is broken.

## Team level and organisation level

These same eight dimensions work at two scales, and they do not always agree. A single team can be healthy inside itself while the organisation around it is fractured, and a company can look aligned at the top while individual teams quietly are not. Trust within a team is a different thing from trust between teams. Belonging to your immediate group is different from belonging to the company. We read both, because fixing a team and fixing the organisation it sits in are related but separate jobs, and confusing them is how good interventions land in the wrong place.

## What the dimensions are for

Naming the eight does one practical thing above all: it turns "the team feels off" into "decision-making has stalled and belonging is thin," which is a problem you can design for and measure against. That is the whole use of them. They are not a philosophy of teamwork. They are the vocabulary that lets you find what is wrong, aim an experience at it, and check afterwards whether it moved.

Everything else we write about — the moments, the roles, the functions, the industries — comes back to these eight. They are the ground the rest of it stands on.
