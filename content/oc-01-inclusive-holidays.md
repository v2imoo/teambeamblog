Holidays are a test of whether a company actually means what it says about belonging, and most companies quietly fail it in one of two ways. Either they ignore the calendar entirely, treating every occasion as a day off and nothing more, which reads as cold. Or they celebrate some traditions warmly and pass over others, which sends a message no one intends but everyone notices: whose culture is the default here, and whose is a guest.

There is a way through that avoids both, and it starts with being clear about what a workplace is actually marking when it marks a holiday.

## Mark the value, not the belief

Under almost every occasion worth marking is a human value that does not belong to any one group. A festival of light is, underneath, about hope and renewal. A harvest festival is about gratitude and the reward of shared effort. A remembrance day is about honouring what came before. A new year is about beginning again. These values are the reason the occasions endure, and they are shared by people who hold very different beliefs about everything else.

A workplace is not a place of worship, and it does not need to take a position on anyone's theology to mark these values. It can mark the gratitude without the doctrine, the renewal without the ritual claim, the light without the specific god. Done well, this is not a watering-down. It is finding the part of the occasion that everyone in the room can genuinely share, and gathering around that.

## Why this builds belonging rather than dividing

The reason this matters is belonging, which is one of the eight dimensions of a healthy team and one of the most sensitive. Belonging is damaged when someone looks at how their company celebrates and sees that their moments are invisible while others are marked, or that they are expected to participate in something that is not theirs while their own passes unnoticed. It is built when everyone can look at the year and see their moments acknowledged, and can join in others' without being made to feel like an outsider or a token.

Marking the shared value under each occasion does both at once. It lets a company acknowledge a wide range of traditions without ranking them, and it gives everyone a genuine way in, because gratitude and renewal and remembrance are things a whole team can feel together regardless of what they believe.

## The mistakes to avoid

Two failures are common even among companies trying to do this well. The first is tokenism — marking an occasion with a poster and a themed lunch and nothing real, which people read instantly as a box being ticked. The second is asking employees from a given background to perform their culture for everyone else, which puts the burden of inclusion on the very people it is meant to include. Both do more harm than doing nothing.

The alternative is to mark occasions the way you would mark anything that matters to a team: with genuine shared experience built around the value everyone can hold, run so that people feel included rather than catalogued.

## Belonging is the measure

The point of marking the calendar is not the marking. It is whether people feel more part of the team afterward, or less. That is a belonging question, and belonging can be read. A company that marks holidays inclusively is not doing it for the photos. It is doing it because a team where everyone sees their moments acknowledged is a team where more people feel they belong — and belonging is one of the quiet dimensions that decides who stays and who contributes.

Mark the value, include everyone in it, and the occasion does what it is supposed to do.
