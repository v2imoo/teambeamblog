Every company has one team whose health quietly determines all the others, and it is usually the one no one is allowed to examine. The executive team is a team. It has the same eight dimensions as any other — trust, communication, alignment, decision-making, belonging, and the rest — and it succeeds or fails on them just like a project team does. But it is rarely treated as a team that needs work, and that omission is expensive, because whatever is true of the team at the top becomes true of the organisation beneath it.

## The weather cascades

The executive team sets the weather. Not through what it announces, but through how it actually operates, which everyone below reads and copies whether they mean to or not.

If the executives do not trust each other, their organisations will not trust each other, because each leader carries the wariness back to their own team. If the top team cannot make a decision and stay decided, decisions will be reopened at every level below. If two executives are quietly at war, their departments will be too, and the people caught between them will spend their energy managing a conflict they did not start. If the leadership team is aligned in the room and contradictory outside it, the whole company gets mixed signals and learns to wait rather than move.

None of this is fixed by strategy offsites about the business. It is fixed, or not fixed, by the health of the team having the conversation.

## Why it is the hardest team to work on

The executive team is also the team it is hardest to help, for reasons that are entirely human. Power makes vulnerability costly, so the trust that requires being exposed is scarce exactly where the stakes are highest. Ego is more concentrated. Time is guarded, so the team almost never steps back to look at itself. And there is a quiet assumption that people senior enough to run functions do not need to work on being a team, which is precisely backwards — they need it most, because their dysfunction is the most expensive.

So the top team tends to be the least developed team in the company, running on the assumption that seniority substitutes for cohesion. It does not. A group of individually excellent leaders who do not trust or align is a weak team, and a weak team at the top is a weak organisation with good slides.

## Where the leverage is

This is why the executive team is the highest-leverage place to work, not the lowest. A ten-percent improvement in trust or alignment among a project team helps that project. The same improvement in the leadership team changes the conditions every team in the company operates under. The cascade runs both ways: dysfunction replicates downward, and so does health.

Working on it means treating the executives as a team with the honesty that requires — surfacing the trust that is missing, the alignment that is assumed but absent, the decisions that keep coming undone — in a setting where senior people can actually be exposed without it being used against them. That is delicate work, and it is the work that moves the most.

## Reading the top

The leadership team can be read like any other, across the same dimensions, and the reading is often the most revealing one a company can take, because it explains so much of what happens below. A CEO who understands the health of their own team understands the source of half the patterns they see across the organisation.

The team at the top is the one team you cannot afford to leave unexamined, precisely because everyone else is watching it to learn how to be a team.
