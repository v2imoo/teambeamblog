Before we design anything, we read the team. We call it a scan, and it is the step most team building skips entirely. The booking comes first, the team's actual state is guessed at, and the guess is usually the manager's guess, which is one person's view of a system they sit inside.

A scan replaces the guess with a reading. It is worth being concrete about what that means and why it changes the answer.

## Reading, not asking

The obvious way to find out what a team needs is to ask the person who runs it. That is a useful input and a partial one. A manager sees the team from a single vantage point, and it is the vantage point with the most power in the room, which is exactly the position from which certain problems are invisible.

The person who stopped speaking up in meetings knows something the manager does not. The two functions that quietly route around each other know something the manager does not. The new joiner who still feels like a guest knows something the manager does not. A scan reads the team as a whole, across the people in it, so the picture is not just the view from the top.

## What we read for

We read a team across eight dimensions: trust, communication, alignment, collaboration, decision-making, energy, belonging, and leadership. We are not going to describe how the reading is taken, because that is the craft and it stays in the work. What matters here is the shape of what comes back.

A team is rarely weak everywhere or strong everywhere. It is usually strong on some dimensions and quietly hollow on one or two, and the hollow ones are doing most of the damage. A team can communicate constantly and still not trust each other. It can be full of energy and completely unaligned. The scan is what separates the dimension that looks like the problem from the dimension that is the problem.

## The answer is almost never what was expected

Here is the pattern we see most. A leader comes in certain of the diagnosis. The team needs more fun. The team needs to bond. The team is low on energy. Sometimes that is right. Often the scan finds something underneath it. The team that "needs to bond" turns out to trust each other fine and simply cannot make a decision without it being relitigated a week later. The team that "needs energy" turns out to be perfectly capable and quietly excluded from anything that matters, which is not an energy problem, it is a belonging one.

If you design for the expected problem, you build a good day for a problem the team did not have. If you design for the real one, you have a chance of changing something. That gap is the entire reason the scan exists.

## Why this is the honest step

A scan also does something uncomfortable and useful: it makes the goal explicit before anyone commits to an activity. Once you have read the team, you know what you are trying to move, which means you can be held to whether you moved it. That is the opposite of the usual arrangement, where the goal stays vague enough that any pleasant day counts as success.

We would rather start with a clear reading and a clear target, because it is the only way the measurement afterwards means anything. You cannot prove you fixed a problem you never named.

Everything downstream — the design, the delivery, the proof — rests on this first step. Read the team, find the dimension that is actually costing it, and design for that. Skip it, and you are decorating a guess.
